\begin{abstract}
The widespread adoption of cloud computing technologies enabled notable consolidation of computational resources and unprecedented service agility, with Kubernetes (K8s) as the default open-source container orchestration platform that automates application management. However, managing K8s clusters at scale demands continuous monitoring, accurate resource forecasting, and timely autoscaling decisions---tasks that exceed the capacity of static, threshold-based controllers. To efficiently orchestrate K8s operations, we present \Solution, an agentic AI platform that combines Large Language Model (LLM) reasoning with a more formal multi-criteria optimization. The system ingests real-time cluster metrics %through the Kubernetes Metrics API 
and feeds them into a predictive monitoring pipeline comprising linear-trend forecasting with %bootstrap-based 
uncertainty quantification and %Isolation-Forest 
anomaly detection. %calibrated via Platt scaling.
A context-aware LLM advisor analyzes forecasts alongside deployment state and generates scaling recommendations (i.e., suggestions to scale up or down) for both Horizontal Pod Autoscaler (HPA) and Vertical Pod Autoscaler (VPA) resources. Each LLM recommendation is cross-validated by a %TOPSIS-based 
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) module that evaluates candidate scaling actions against performance and cost criteria %five criteria---cost, performance, stability, forecast alignment, and response time---and can override the LLM when the MCDA dominance margin exceeds a configurable threshold.
and drives LLM towards a more stable configuration.
%All components are exposed through a web dashboard that visualizes prediction intervals, exceedance probabilities, uncertainty decomposition, and calibrated anomaly severities, enabling operators to assess both the scaling decision and its confidence.
As we envision the cloud operator as responsible for such decisions, we present these recommendations (along with predictions and uncertainty estimates) via a web API for the final decision.
We deployed this solution over a local testbed and demonstrated end-to-end agentic autoscaling with transparent, auditable decision-making.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Traditional cloud computing operations often require complex manual configurations, especially for deploying services in containerized environments (\textit{e.g.,} microservices). Kubernetes (K8s) has become the de facto open-source platform for container orchestration, enabling automated software deployment, scaling, and management. However, defining deployment specifications, such as resource requests and limits, still demands substantial expertise and remains challenging even for experienced practitioners~\cite{yu2020microscaler, al2024containerized, sacco2025intent}.
%In particular, despite the automation provided by the K8s suite, operations like capacity planning, resource optimization, and scaling remain challenging. This is due to the complexity of tasks that are required to achieve a system that meets provisioning targets reliably. Examples of these tasks are predicting resource demands, optimal QoS-compliant workload packing, stable scaling that accounts for dependencies among services and application statefulness, gathering telemetry, and safeguarding automated operations.
In particular, despite K8s's built-in automation, reliably meeting provisioning targets remains complex, requiring accurate demand forecasting, QoS-aware workload packing, dependency- and state-aware scaling, robust telemetry collection, and safeguards for automated operations.
%
%Overcoming these hurdles can prove both labor-intensive and difficult with traditional tools as infrastructures scale massively. For this reason, integrating Agentic Artificial Intelligence (Agentic AI) into orchestration workflows has become the focus of many research efforts and has led to the emergence of AIOps~\cite{shetty2024building}, a design paradigm that shifts from predominantly human-driven processes toward the autonomous AI-enabled management of complex IT and cloud infrastructures. The incorporation of AI-driven techniques into large-scale cloud infrastructure management, which is comprised of a multitude of complex workflows, introduces both substantial opportunities and non-trivial challenges. Embedding AI models within these workflows can assist with high-dimensional telemetry analysis, workload prediction, and adaptive resource optimization, capabilities that are increasingly difficult to achieve using traditional mechanisms alone. In the specific case of capacity management, traditional threshold-based and reactive scaling mechanisms are often insufficient to handle dynamic traffic patterns and the delayed effects of scaling decisions, both inherent to distributed systems. These limitations motivate the exploration of AI-driven resource management approaches for k8s capacity planning, leveraging the strengths of reasoning-capable models to enable proactive management by factoring in application behavior, container-level resource specifications, and cluster-level scheduling dynamics when devising capacity plans.
Addressing these challenges with traditional tools becomes increasingly labor-intensive, motivating the integration of Agentic AI into orchestration workflows under the AIOps paradigm~\cite{shetty2024building}. This shift toward autonomous and AI-enabled management enables advanced telemetry analysis, workload prediction, and adaptive resource optimization across complex cloud environments. In capacity management specifically, traditional mechanisms, \textit{i.e.,} reactive and threshold-based scaling~\cite{yu2020microscaler, rossi2020self, rossi2020geo, xu2025auto, ahmad2025towards}, often fail to cope with dynamic traffic and delayed scaling effects in distributed systems. This prompts the adoption of AI-driven approaches for K8s planning that incorporate application behavior, container-level specifications, and cluster-level scheduling dynamics to enable proactive, context-aware resource management~\cite{khaleq2021intelligent, nguyen2022graph, song2023chainsformer, subramaniam2023automated, santos2023gym, quattrocchi2024autoscaling}.

%Nonetheless, the practical adoption of AIOps and agentic AI within K8s-based infrastructures raises several technical challenges. First, identifying appropriate operational use cases remains non-trivial, as not all tasks benefit equally from learning-based automation. Second, effective model performance depends on the availability and quality of observability data, such as metrics, logs, traces, and events collected across clusters. Data sparsity, noise, and cross-layer dependencies can significantly limit model reliability. Third, AI systems must be tightly integrated with control-plane APIs and policy frameworks to safely and timely actuate changes in live environments. This requirement introduces concerns related to model deployment in resource-constrained clusters, scalability across multi-cluster or multi-cloud infrastructures, and the interpretability of AI-generated decisions—particularly when those decisions affect service availability or compliance constraints.

Consequently, AI-driven control loops must incorporate safeguard mechanisms such as policy validation, rollback strategies, bounded action spaces, and, where appropriate, human-in-the-loop supervision. Ensuring transparency, auditability, and explainability of automated decisions remains a critical requirement for operational trust and regulatory alignment.
Nonetheless, the practical adoption of AIOps and agentic AI within K8s-based infrastructures raises several key technical challenges:

\textit{(i)} First, not all operational responsibilities are appropriate candidates for AI-driven automation, so selecting the right use cases is essential;
\textit{(ii)} Second, the effectiveness of any model is inherently tied to the quality of the underlying observability data. Noisy, incomplete, or inconsistent metrics, logs, and traces can significantly degrade predictive performance and decision reliability;

\textit{(iii)} Third, safe operations in production environments require tight integration with K8s control-plane APIs and policy mechanisms, particularly when automated decisions directly trigger scaling or configuration changes;
and \textit{(iii)} lastly, when AI-driven decisions impact the system's availability, the need for transparency, explainability, and auditability becomes essential to ensure trustworthy operation and accountable decision-making.
Increasing agent autonomy amplifies safety concerns, where erroneous scaling actions, configuration updates, or remediation procedures may result in cascading failures, service disruption, or SLA violations for end users. This challenge introduces the need for safeguards such as policy validation, rollback mechanisms, bounded action spaces, human-in-the-loop supervision (where deemed), and strong transparency, auditability, and explainability.

To address these challenges, we present \Solution{}, our agentic AI platform designed for autonomous capacity management in K8s environments. \Solution{} leverages large language models (LLMs) to produce proactive, context-aware auto-scaling decisions informed by short-term resource utilization forecasting. Unlike reactive threshold-based approaches, our system anticipates workload dynamics and adapts replica counts accordingly. To ensure reliability and operational safety, \Solution{} incorporates explicit guardrails via controlled decision policies, and optional human-in-the-loop approval mechanisms. Through an experimental campaign, we demonstrate that the platform accurately forecasts resource demand and effectively translates these predictions into scaling actions that maintain SLA compliance while reducing cloud infrastructure costs.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Recent advances in AIOps increasingly position LLMs as the central reasoning component in cloud infrastructure management, complementing or replacing traditional machine learning (ML) (e.g.~\cite{liu2022scanflow}) and human-centered pipelines. Beyond predictive analytics, this shift reflects the emergence of agentic AI systems capable of autonomous reasoning, tool use, and iterative decision-making across operational workflows.

Existing literature primarily focuses on failure and incident management in cloud platforms~\cite{zhang2025survey}. In this context, AI agents have been explored for anomaly detection, root cause analysis (RCA), and automated failure mitigation, with growing industrial adoption. Unlike static ML models, agentic systems can dynamically interpret logs, query external knowledge sources, and orchestrate remediation actions, enabling more adaptive and context-aware operations.
%
For instance,~\cite{delacruzcabello2025aiops} introduces an LLM-based framework for log anomaly detection, showing that with augmentation techniques such as fine-tuning, RAG, prompt engineering, or ensembles, LLMs can surpass traditional methods. \section{System Model}
\label{sec:system}

\subsection{\Solution\ Overview}

Agentic AIOps represents the shift from passive observability to autonomous operational intelligence by introducing goal-driven, decision-making agents that can interpret system state, reason about potential actions, and safely execute changes in production environments. 
In Kubernetes systems, Agentic AI systems can continuously analyze telemetry, predict capacity, and take corrective actions such as scaling workloads, while adhering to guardrails. 
%
Figure~\ref{fig:overview} illustrates \Solution, our solution for intelligent, context-aware auto-scaling operations in containerized infrastructures. 

\Solution\ is a modular platform that operates \emph{outside} the managed
K8s cluster and connects through standard \texttt{kubeconfig} credentials. This design avoids intrusive modifications to the cluster while preserving full compatibility with native K8s APIs.
At runtime, \Solution\ retrieves cluster metrics (i.e., CPU and memory utilization), generates short-term load forecasts, and submits the aggregated contextual information to the LLM-based advisor to obtain a scaling recommendation. The proposed action is subsequently evaluated by a Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) module, which verifies policy compliance, safety constraints, and operational limits. If necessary, MCDA may adjust or override the LLM’s recommendation.
Once validated, the final scaling decision is applied to the K8s cluster 
%via standard \texttt{kubectl patch} or \texttt{kubectl scale} commands.
that enforces the change through its native control-plane reconciliation mechanism, ensuring that the desired state specified in the resource definition is eventually realized by the running system.
%
The system supports both Horizontal Pod Autoscaling (HPA) and Vertical Pod Autoscaling (VPA). HPA dynamically adjusts the number of pod replicas based on observed resource utilization or custom metrics, thereby scaling workloads horizontally. In contrast, VPA modifies the CPU and memory resource requests (and optionally limits) of individual pods, effectively scaling workloads vertically by resizing containers rather than changing replica counts.

\subsection{Metrics Collector}
\label{sec:metrics}

The \emph{Metrics Collector} gathers real-time resource telemetry from the target K8s cluster.  Rather than requiring a full monitoring stack such as Prometheus, \Solution{} queries only a lightweight Kubernetes Metrics Server~\cite{k8s_metrics_server,k8s_resource_metrics_pipeline,k8s_kubectl_top}, a scalable and efficient plugin that operates a source of container resource metrics for autoscaling pipelines.
In such a way, our approach works with any standard cluster, eliminating the need to deploy additional infrastructure.  
The collector retrieves metrics using a prioritized, sequential strategy. It first attempts to obtain metrics using a primary method. If this attempt fails or does not return within a fixed timeout ($10$\,s), the system proceeds to the next method in the predefined order. This process continues until metrics are successfully retrieved or all available methods have been attempted. 
%Each individual metrics request is allowed to run for at most 10\,s. 
Individual requests that exceed this timeout are treated as failed, triggering the next method in the sequence. This design ensures predictable latency while providing robustness against transient failures or slow responses from individual metric sources.
%
The collector attempts to retrieve metrics from the following sources, in order of priority:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Kubectl top.}  The primary method executes \texttt{kubectl top nodes} as a subprocess and parses the tabular output for per-node CPU (millicores) and memory (MiB). This service internally reads from the Metrics Server but returns values in a format similar to the traditional \texttt{top} command. We have empirically observed that on a single-node testbed, this call completes in ${\sim}200$--$500$\,ms, which makes our timeout value a valid threshold.
    %\pn{In our single-node CrownLabs testbed, this call typically completed in a few hundred milliseconds (empirical observation).}
    \item \textbf{Metrics API.}  If \texttt{kubectl top} fails or is unavailable, the collector queries via direct API requests to the Metrics Server. The endpoint \texttt{metrics.\allowbreak{}k8s.\allowbreak{}io/v1beta1} is accessed via a Kubernetes Python client, %(\texttt{CustomObjectsApi}), 
    converting raw millicore and byte values to percentages using each node's allocatable capacity. 
    \item \textbf{Kubelet Summary API.}  As a last resort, the collector queries each node's Kubelet via the API server proxy that returns \texttt{usageNanoCores}. This value represents how much CPU a workload is using, thus we convert it to CPU usage using the formula 1\,CPU\,$=$\,$10^{9}$\,nanocores.
\end{enumerate}

The methods are not executed in parallel since only one method is active per collection cycle; the fallback is transparent to downstream consumers.  Raw values are normalized to cluster-wide utilization percentages: $\text{CPU\%} = \text{usage} / \text{allocatable} \times 100$, and analogously for memory.  
The \emph{Metrics Collector} produces an aggregated system snapshot that summarizes the current operational state. This snapshot includes CPU and memory utilization (in percent), the \emph{pod count} (i.e., the number of active application instances running for the monitored workload), and the \emph{node count} (i.e., the number of worker machines currently available in the Kubernetes cluster, reflecting overall cluster capacity).
In addition, the snapshot captures \emph{network I/O}, defined as the volume of data transmitted and received over the network, and \emph{disk I/O}, representing the amount of data read from and written to persistent storage.
%Together, these metrics provide a multi-dimensional view of compute, capacity, communication, and storage pressure within the system. 
With a time interval $\Delta t$, the module generates a matrix using the last $N$ samples in a rolling in-memory window to feed the forecasting module. By default, we set $\Delta t =300$\,s, but this value can be customized for faster or slower decisions.

\subsection{Predictive Monitoring}
\label{sec:prediction}

\subsubsection{Time-Series Forecasting.}
Using the collected metrics, we predict future CPU and RAM utilization with a regression algorithm that forecasts future values based on historical and evolving trends. The history feeds a one-step-ahead AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model \cite{box2015timeseries}, a widely used statistical framework for time-series forecasting, chosen for its low deployment requirements and low latency.
An ARIMA model is denoted as ARIMA$(p,d,q)$, where $p$ represents the order of the autoregressive (AR) component (i.e., the number of lag observations included in the model), 
$d$ denotes the degree of differencing required to achieve stationarity (i.e., the number of times that the raw observations are differenced), 
and $q$ represents the order of the moving average (MA) component (i.e., the size of the moving average window).
In our tests, we set $p = 3$, $d = 0$, and $q = 0$ after performing parameter optimization via grid search.

\subsubsection{Bootstrap Uncertainty Quantification.}
\label{subsec:uncert}

Point forecasts alone cannot express how much trust an operator should place in a prediction.  \Solution{} therefore wraps every forecast in a \emph{prediction interval} produced by bootstrap resampling~\cite{efron1993introduction}.

Given $N$ historical observations, uncertainty is quantified via a nonparametric bootstrap procedure. Specifically, $B = 50$ bootstrap samples, each of size $N$, are drawn with replacement from the original dataset. For each bootstrap replicate $b \in \{1,\dots,B\}$, a linear model is fitted and $h$-step-ahead predictions $\hat{y}^{(b)}(t+h)$ are generated.

The epistemic (model) uncertainty at forecast horizon $h$ is estimated as the empirical variance across bootstrap predictions,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:epistemic} \sigma^2_{\mathrm{epi}}(h) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \bigl(\hat{y}^{(b)}(t+h) - \bar{y}(t+h)\bigr)^{2}, 
\end{equation}
where $\bar{y}(t+h) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\hat{y}^{(b)}(t+h)$ denotes the mean bootstrap forecast. This quantity captures variability due to finite-sample effects and parameter uncertainty.

The total predictive variance is assumed to increase with the forecast horizon and is modeled as:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:total_var}
  \sigma^2(h) = \sigma^2_{\mathrm{ale}} \cdot \sqrt{h}
              + \sigma^2_{\mathrm{epi}} \cdot h,
\end{equation}
This formulation reflects the intuition that both observational noise and model uncertainty accumulate over time.

Assuming approximate normality of the predictive distribution, a prediction interval with a confidence $100(1-\alpha)\%$ is constructed as:
%expected range of future utilization
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pred_interval}
  \hat{y}(t+h) \pm z_{\alpha/2}\,\sigma(h),
\end{equation}
where $z_{\alpha/2}$ is the standard normal quantile (e.g., $z_{0.025}=1.96$ for a $95\%$ prediction interval). The prediction interval provides a range of plausible values for future utilization, thereby quantifying forecast uncertainty rather than relying solely on a single point estimate.

In addition, the system computes four \emph{exceedance probabilities} for $4$ predefined utilization thresholds $\tau \in \{50, 70, 80, 90 \}\%$, denoting the likelihood that utilization exceeds each threshold. The probabilities are obtained in the form of $\Pr\!\left(\hat{y}(t+h) > \tau\right)$.
These probabilities are obtained using the complementary cumulative distribution function of the normal distribution with mean $\hat{y}(t+h)$ and variance $\sigma^2(h)$. %They quantify the risk that the predicted value exceeds operationally relevant capacity limits and can be used for proactive alerting or resource management decisions.
These values directly reflect operational risk (e.g., the probability of entering high- or critical-utilization zones) and are used for dashboard visualizations, proactive alerting, and scaling decisions.
Elevated exceedance probabilities at higher thresholds indicate an increased risk of capacity saturation and may therefore justify more aggressive scale-up actions. Conversely, low exceedance probabilities suggest limited risk, supporting decisions to maintain the current allocation or to scale down resources.
%higher exceedance at high thresholds means higher risk and can trigger stronger scale-up recommendations, while low exceedance supports maintain/scale-down decisions. 

\subsubsection{Calibrated Anomaly Detection.}

In addition to resource forecasting, we provide an anomaly indication to facilitate operator interpretation and provide additional contextual signals for automated scaling and resource management decisions.
%
An Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008isolation} with contamination parameter
$c = 0.1$ is employed to detect anomalous behavior. The model produces a
raw anomaly score $s \in [-1,1]$, where larger values indicate a higher
degree of abnormality. However, these raw scores are not directly
interpretable as calibrated probabilities and are therefore difficult to
use for risk-aware decision making.

To obtain probabilistic outputs, \Solution{} applies \emph{Platt scaling}~\cite{platt1999probabilistic}, transforming the raw score into an estimated anomaly probability via a logistic mapping:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:platt}
  \Pr(\text{anomaly} \mid s) = \sigma(As + B)
    = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(As + B)}},
\end{equation}
where $A = -5.0$ and $B = -0.5$ are learned calibration parameters. This transformation maps the Isolation Forest score to the interval $[0,1]$, yielding a quantity that can be interpreted as the estimated likelihood of anomalous behavior. Compared to raw scores, calibrated probabilities are easier to interpret, monitor over time, and integrate into downstream decision logic.

The resulting probability is further mapped to a discrete severity distribution across four levels (low, medium, high, critical), enabling intuitive visualization and prioritization in operational dashboards.
In addition, a detection confidence measure is defined as $\gamma = 2\,\bigl|P(\text{anomaly}) - 0.5\bigr|,$
%The value 0.5 is the neutral midpoint of a calibrated probability in $[0,1]$. At $P(\text{anomaly})=0.5$, uncertainty is maximal and $\gamma=0$; as $P(\text{anomaly})$ moves toward 0 or 1, confidence increases and $\gamma$ approaches 1. So 0.5 is not a tuned threshold, but the natural center of the probability scale.}
which quantifies the distance from maximal uncertainty. Values of $\gamma$ close to $1$ indicate high confidence ($P(\text{anomaly})$ near $0$ or $1$), whereas values close to $0$ correspond to uncertain detections.
%
Overall, this calibration and post-processing pipeline converts anomaly detection from a binary indicator into a probabilistic risk score with associated severity and confidence measures.

\section{LLM-Based Autoscaling Advisor}
\label{sec:llm}

The autoscaling advisor formulates scaling as a structured natural-language reasoning task. Rather than relying solely on predefined threshold rules, the system aggregates relevant operational signals into a unified prompt and delegates the final recommendation to an LLM. This design enables context-aware decisions that jointly consider workload dynamics, resource constraints, and deployment characteristics.

For each evaluation cycle, a structured prompt is constructed from four sources of contextual information:
(i)~\emph{current runtime metrics}, including CPU and memory utilization, pod count, and the number of ready and available pods;
(ii)~\emph{forecast information}, comprising the current value, predicted peak, overall trend, and per-hour predictions over a horizon of $H = 6$ hours;
(iii)~\emph{deployment configuration and state}, such as the current number of replicas, minimum and maximum scaling bounds, resource requests and limits, and the status of Horizontal Pod Autoscaling (HPA) and Vertical Pod Autoscaling (VPA); and
(iv)~\emph{statefulness classification}, which characterizes whether the workload can be safely scaled horizontally (described below).

Given this structured context, the LLM produces a machine-readable JSON response specifying: (1) the recommended \texttt{action} (\texttt{scale\_up}, \texttt{scale\_down}, or \texttt{maintain}); (2) the \texttt{scaling\_type} (\texttt{hpa} or \texttt{vpa}); (3) the target number of replicas or updated VPA resource requests; (4) a confidence score; and (5) a free-text rationale explaining the decision. This output format ensures interpretability for operators while remaining directly consumable by automated control components.

\subsubsection{Model Selection and Caching.}

\Solution{} implements a two-tier LLM fallback strategy to ensure reliable scaling decisions. The primary model is a self-hosted open-source LLM (e.g., Qwen) accessed via an OpenAI-compatible endpoint, configured with a $240$\,s timeout and low generation temperature ($\theta = 0.1$) to encourage stable, deterministic outputs. If unavailable, the system automatically falls back to a cloud-based model via Groq API (e.g., Llama~3.1-8B) with a $15$\,s timeout to maintain responsiveness.
%
To reduce operational cost and redundant inference, recommendations are cached for a configurable TTL (default $300$\,s), and LLM calls are rate-limited to a minimum interval of $30$\,s. This design balances robustness, latency, and cost efficiency.

\subsubsection{HPA vs.\ VPA Decision.}\label{sec:hpa-vpa}
Selecting between horizontal and vertical scaling is a critical design decision. Scaling out via HPA may be unsafe for stateful applications that maintain in-pod session data, potentially leading to data inconsistency or loss. Conversely, scaling up via VPA for a stateless service can be inefficient, as additional capacity could instead be distributed across replicas.
To address this, \Solution{} determines the appropriate scaling strategy through a three-stage decision process.

\paragraph{Stage~1: Statefulness Detection.}

Before calling the LLM, \Solution{} determines whether a workload should be treated as stateful or stateless. This distinction is critical, as it directly influences whether HPA or vertical VPA scaling is appropriate.
The classification is derived from a structured analysis of the Deployment specification, including: operator-provided annotations, environment variables, storage configuration about persistent volumes, or the presence of a database in the namespace. These signals are evaluated in priority order and aggregated into a statefulness label with an associated confidence level.
The resulting classification is included in the LLM prompt to ensure that scaling recommendations respect workload semantics and avoid unsafe scaling actions.

\paragraph{Stage~2: LLM Reasoning.}
Using this insight, the LLM returns a corresponding autoscaling decision. Because the LLM is provided with the complete deployment context (e.g., replica limits, resource bounds, and forecasted load), it can make context-aware trade-offs. For example, even for a stateless application, the model may recommend VPA if the workload has already reached its maximum replica count and further horizontal scaling is not feasible.

\paragraph{Stage~3: Post-Processing Enforcement.}
LLM outputs can be inconsistent: the reasoning text may mention ``state stored
inside the pod'' while the JSON sets \texttt{scaling\_type:~hpa}.  A
rule-based enforcement layer catches such contradictions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item If the reasoning contains stateful indicators but the output is HPA, the system corrects to VPA and computes the target CPU/memory from the current usage plus a 20\% headroom.
  \item If the detection classifies the workload as stateless, but the LLM outputs VPA, the system forces HPA and recalculates target replicas from current metrics and forecast peaks.
  \item If no statefulness information is available and the LLM chose HPA,
        the system defaults to VPA as the safer option.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Scaling Actuation.}
Once the scaling type and targets are finalised, the corresponding engine module applies the change:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{HPA:} a JSON Patch sets \texttt{minReplicas} and
        \texttt{maxReplicas} on the HPA resource
        (\texttt{kubectl patch hpa --type=json}).
  \item \textbf{VPA:} a strategic merge patch updates the container's \\
        \texttt{resources.requests} in the Deployment spec
        (\texttt{kubectl patch deployment --type=merge}).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{MCDA Cross-Validation}
\label{sec:mcda}

LLM recommendations are inherently non-deterministic.  To provide a formal safeguard, every recommendation is cross-validated by a Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) module that implements the Technique for Order of Preference by Similarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS)~\cite{hwang1981topsis}.

\paragraph{Alternative Generation.}
Given the current replica count $r$, the module generates a set of candidate scaling alternatives. Specifically, it considers $r + \delta$ for $\delta \in \{-2,-1,0,+1,+2,+3\}$, the minimum and maximum allowable replica counts, and an ``ideal'' replica count that would yield a target CPU utilization of 70\%. Candidates falling outside the admissible range $[\textit{min},\textit{max}]$ are discarded. This procedure typically produces between five and eight feasible alternatives.

\paragraph{Criteria Evaluation.}
Each feasible alternative is evaluated against five decision criteria (Table~\ref{tab:criteria}). Criterion values are normalized and weighted, and the resulting decision matrix is then processed using the TOPSIS method.

\paragraph{TOPSIS Ranking.}
Let $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ denote the decision matrix with $m$ alternatives and $n=5$ criteria. TOPSIS is applied as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Normalization:} $r_{ij} = \frac{x_{ij}}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{m} x_{ij}^2}}.$
  \item \textbf{Weighting:} $v_{ij} = w_j \cdot r_{ij},$
        where $\mathbf{w}$ corresponds to one of four predefined weight profiles
        (balanced, performance-first, cost-optimised, stability-first).
  \item \textbf{Ideal and anti-ideal solutions:}
        For each criterion $j$,
        \[
        A^{+}_j =
        \begin{cases}
        \max_i v_{ij}, & \text{benefit criterion}, \\
        \min_i v_{ij}, & \text{cost criterion},
        \end{cases}
        \qquad
        A^{-}_j \text{ defined conversely.}
        \]
  \item \textbf{Separation measures:}
        $D^{+}_i = \lVert \mathbf{v}_i - A^{+} \rVert_2$, \; $D^{-}_i = \lVert \mathbf{v}_i - A^{-} \rVert_2$.
  \item \textbf{Closeness coefficient:}
        $C_i = D^{-}_i / (D^{+}_i + D^{-}_i) \in [0,1]$.
\end{enumerate}

The alternative with the highest $C_i$ is selected as the MCDA-optimal scaling action.

\paragraph{Agreement and Override Mechanism.}
The MCDA module compares the LLM-recommended target replica count $r_L$ with the MCDA-optimal target $r_M$. Agreement is categorised into three levels:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Full agreement:} identical scaling direction and 
        $|r_L - r_M| \leq 1$.
  \item \textbf{Partial agreement:} identical scaling direction but different magnitude.
  \item \textbf{Divergence:} opposite scaling directions.
\end{itemize}
In cases of partial agreement or divergence, the system evaluates the difference between the respective closeness scores. If the MCDA closeness exceeds the LLM’s score by more than a predefined threshold $\delta = 0.15$, the MCDA-optimal action overrides the LLM recommendation.
The validation outcome (including agreement level, both closeness scores, dominance margin, applied weight profile, and full ranking) is attached to the final recommendation and presented via the dashboard.

Four predefined weight profiles enable operators to encode different operational priorities (Table~\ref{tab:weights}). The default configuration is the \emph{balanced} profile.

Predictably, the results indicate that our solution introduces overhead in the control loop, with native controllers taking less time to react,
than \Solution{} does for making a predictive decision, due to LLM inference and MCDA
validation in the control loop. Across the baseline
campaign, \Solution{} produced recommendations in all runs and applied
replica changes in \(3/10\) runs.

We further evaluate \Solution{} by quantifying service-level compliance. We perform SLA assessment through $N=20$ request-path probes per run, made with in-cluster HTTP
requests to the application endpoint. A request is counted as an SLA violation if either HTTP
status is \(\ge 400\) or latency exceeds the threshold (\(0.5\)~s). 
We set the latency threshold to \(0.5\)~s as an application-level Service Level Objective (SLO) for interactive web response under bursty load: it is strict enough to flag user-visible degradation while remaining realistic for this lightweight HTTP benchmark. It is also applied uniformly across methods to preserve fairness of comparison.
Results of this evaluation are shown in Table~\ref{tab:service-cost}:
we can observe that our solution outperforms both Native HPA and Native VPA, leading to fewer request-level SLA violations for the configured latency threshold (\(0.5\)~s), with request latency being comparable. This remarks how \Solution{}'s predictive nature can bring tangible advantages in terms of service quality.

Table~\ref{tab:service-cost} also shows estimated average cost values over the observation window. Cost is reported here as a \emph{resource proxy}, not direct cloud
billing, computed as the average requested vCPU over the observed window:
$\text{avg\_requested\_vcpu}=\text{avg\_replicas}\times
\text{cpu\_request\_per\_pod}/1000$. While this metric does factor in real provider pricing, discounts, storage, or network charges, it does however provide an indication of relative resource footprint
under each method (higher means larger requested CPU footprint). We can observe how the average vCPU usage is comparable among the three methods, further corroborating the practical usability of our solution.

Additionally, we perform a sensitivity test to isolate the effect of the MCDA gate on responsiveness and outcome quality. We set the MCDA profile to \texttt{performance\_first} (see Table \ref{tab:weights}) and lower the agreement threshold to \(0.05\). We choose \(0.05\) as a targeted stress value to test whether reducing MCDA conservativeness improves actuation frequency and end metrics. Workload profiles and run count are kept identical to previous tests.
Results show that the new conditions increased the replica-change frequency only marginally (\(4/10\) runs) and did not
improve outcome metrics (\Solution{} p95 latency \(0.0297 \pm 0.0072\)~s vs.
\(0.0223 \pm 0.0075\)~s baseline; cost proxy \(1.9796 \pm 0.4526\) vs.
\(1.7675 \pm 0.3724\) requested vCPU). This indicates that simply relaxing override aggressiveness is insufficient to remove the dominant decision-latency bottleneck.

This data supports an important diagnostic conclusion: the primary limitation of our approach is not SLA compliance, but control-loop overhead. In both baseline and
sensitivity runs, request-path reliability remained stable,
while \Solution{}'s decision latency stayed substantially above native-controller
reaction times. This behavior is consistent with a pipeline where inference and
validation overhead dominate short-horizon scenarios.
In order to identify and quantify the latency overhead, we decompose the elapsed decision time of \Solution{}.
Results of this analysis are found in Table\ref{fig:evaluation-autosage-decomp}: in the measured set of runs, metrics collection (\(0.4983 \pm 0.1144\)~s) and LLM
inference (\(1.0008 \pm 0.2178\)~s) are the dominant contributors, while
forecast computation is negligible (\(0.0012 \pm 0.0001\)~s), and actuation
remains comparatively small (\(0.1902 \pm 0.0650\)~s). This results highlights the trade-off of deploying complex LLM-based agentic architectures, in particular between their superior functional performance and the costs associated with their operation.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented \Solution{}, an agentic AI platform for autonomous capacity management in Kubernetes environments. By combining short-term resource utilization forecasting with an LLM, \Solution{} moves beyond reactive, threshold-based auto-scalers toward anticipatory, adaptive scaling strategies. The integration of explicit guardrails, controlled decision policies, and optional human-in-the-loop validation ensures that autonomy is achieved without compromising operational safety or transparency.
%
Our experimental evaluation demonstrates that \Solution{} accurately predicts near-future resource demand and effectively converts these forecasts into scaling actions that maintain SLA compliance while reducing cloud infrastructure costs. These results highlight the potential of agentic AI systems to enhance both performance and efficiency in cloud-native environments.
%
Future work will explore extending the framework to multi-service and multi-cluster scenarios, incorporating additional workload signals and longer forecasting/observation horizons, and further refining alignment mechanisms between LLM-driven recommendations.
